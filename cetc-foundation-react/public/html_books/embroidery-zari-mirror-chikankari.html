Embroidery — Zari, Mirror & Chikankari

📂 Sector: Fashion, Tailoring & Textiles 📊 Level: Foundation ⏱️ Duration: 2 Months 🕐 Hours: 120 💰 Exam Fee: ₹1000
Master three traditional Indian embroidery techniques—Zari (gold thread work), Mirror (shisha embroidery), and Chikankari (white thread work)—in just 8 weeks. This foundation course equips you with hands-on skills to create stunning embellished garments and textiles for the thriving Indian fashion market.

Module 1: Introduction to Embroidery & Textile Fundamentals

📚 Topics Covered

  • History and cultural significance of Zari, Mirror, and Chikankari embroidery in India — Understanding regional variations (Lucknow, Gujarat, Rajasthan)
  • Types of embroidery fabrics — Cotton, silk, chiffon, georgette, net, dupatta, lehenga materials and their properties
  • Fabric grain, weave structure, and thread count — How fabric quality affects embroidery output
  • Introduction to embroidery tools and equipment — Needles, hoops, scissors, thimbles, measuring tools, and their correct usage
  • Safety and hygiene in embroidery work — Eye care, posture, workspace organization, and ergonomic practices
  • Quality standards and defect identification — Common embroidery faults and how to prevent them
  • Indian embroidery market overview — Wedding wear, ethnic clothing, export opportunities, and pricing models
  • Introduction to design reading and pattern understanding — Basic embroidery design symbols and layouts

Module 2: Zari Embroidery Techniques & Metallic Thread Work

📚 Topics Covered

  • Understanding Zari materials — Gold, silver, copper threads, artificial zari, real zari composition, thickness grades (1mm, 1.5mm, 2mm)
  • Types of Zari threads — Flat zari, twisted zari, round zari, and their applications in different garments
  • Zari needle selection and threading techniques — Correct needle sizes (sizes 5-8), thread conditioning, and knot formation
  • Basic Zari stitches — Straight stitch, backstitch, split stitch, running stitch, and long and short stitch using metallic threads
  • Couching technique for Zari work — Laying zari threads and securing with fine thread, creating outlines and fills
  • Zari border designs — Creating traditional borders for dupattas, sarees, lehengas, and suits using couching methods
  • Padding and dimensional Zari work — Building height and texture for wedding embroidery, creating raised designs
  • Zari combined with beads and stones — Attaching kundan, rhinestones, and pearls alongside zari for bridal wear
  • Common Zari defects and corrections — Thread breakage, color fading, tarnishing prevention, and storage methods
  • Zari work on different fabric types — Techniques for silk, georgette, chiffon, and net fabrics

Module 3: Mirror & Shisha Embroidery Techniques

📚 Topics Covered

  • Introduction to Mirror embroidery (Shisha work) — Origin, cultural significance in Rajasthan and Gujarat, mirror types and sizes
  • Mirror materials and preparation — Glass mirrors, acrylic mirrors, thickness selection, cutting, and edge finishing for safety
  • Mirror attachment methods — Traditional frame stitch, modern loop stitch, cross stitch method for securing mirrors to fabric
  • Decorative stitches around mirrors — Chain stitch, blanket stitch, running stitch, and herringbone stitch for aesthetic finish
  • Mirror work on different fabrics — Techniques for cotton, velvet, silk, and ethnic fabrics used in Indian wear
  • Creating mirror work patterns — Geometric designs, floral arrangements, and traditional motifs for suits, dupattas, and ghagras
  • Combining mirrors with other threads — Using silk thread, metallic thread, and beads around mirrors for enhanced designs
  • Mirror work for wedding garments — Bridal lehenga designs, mirror placement for maximum impact, and durability considerations
  • Mirror work troubleshooting — Preventing mirror breakage, loose attachment, and maintaining design integrity during wear and washing
  • Production efficiency in mirror work — Speed techniques, batch processing, and time management for commercial orders

Module 4: Chikankari Embroidery — White Thread Traditional Technique

📚 Topics Covered

  • Understanding Chikankari — Origin in Lucknow, cultural heritage, regional variations, and its role in Indian ethnic fashion
  • Chikankari thread types — White thread grades, cotton thread quality, thread thickness for different stitches, and durability
  • Chikankari on traditional fabrics — Muslin, cotton, linen, chiffon, and georgette used in Lucknowi wear
  • Basic Chikankari stitches — Backstitch (pechni), running stitch (murri), satin stitch (bakhia), chain stitch (zanjira), and French knot
  • Advanced Chikankari stitches — Eyelet work (tepchi), shadow work, cutwork (khidai), and lattice work (jali)
  • Design transfer for Chikankari — Tracing methods, carbon paper technique, and freehand design marking on white fabrics
  • Creating traditional Chikankari motifs — Floral designs, leaf patterns, geometric borders, and corner pieces for dupattas and suits
  • Chikankari on curved surfaces — Necklines, armholes, and shaped garment pieces with consistent stitch quality
  • Combining Chikankari with other techniques — Mixing white thread with colored threads, bead addition, and mirror work integration
  • Quality standards in Chikankari — Stitch uniformity, thread tension, knot hiding, and finishing techniques for export-quality pieces

Module 5: Design, Fabric Preparation & Pattern Transfer

📚 Topics Covered

  • Reading embroidery designs — Understanding design sketches, color coding, stitch symbols, and scale measurements from pattern sheets
  • Design adaptation for different garments — Scaling designs for suits, lehengas, dupattas, sarees, and customized pieces
  • Fabric preparation before embroidery — Washing, blocking, straightening, and marking grain lines for consistent design placement
  • Marking and measuring techniques — Using rulers, measuring tapes, chalk, pencils, and stencils for accurate design placement
  • Pattern transfer methods — Tracing paper method, carbon transfer, light box technique, and prick and pounce method for detailed designs
  • Marking for Zari work — Outlining areas for couching, identifying padding zones, and marking stone/bead placement points
  • Marking for Mirror work — Positioning mirrors on fabric, marking attachment points, and planning decorative stitch placement
  • Marking for Chikankari — Light pencil marks for white thread work, maintaining visibility on white fabrics, and erasing marks after completion
  • Fabric stabilization — Using backing materials, hooping techniques, and support fabrics to prevent puckering and distortion
  • Design planning for production — Batch marking, efficiency layouts, and minimizing fabric waste in commercial embroidery

Module 6: Finishing, Quality Control & Career Development

📚 Topics Covered

  • Finishing embroidered pieces — Removing backing materials, pressing embroidery safely, and final stitch securing techniques
  • Knot hiding and thread securing — Professional methods to hide thread ends, secure stitches, and prevent unraveling during wear
  • Embroidery care and maintenance — Washing instructions, storage methods, and preventing color bleeding and thread damage
  • Quality inspection checklist — Stitch uniformity, design accuracy, thread tension consistency, and defect identification in finished pieces
  • Identifying and correcting common defects — Loose stitches, thread breakage, color fading, mirror breakage, and incomplete designs
  • Production costing and pricing — Calculating material costs, labor time, overhead, and setting competitive prices for embroidery services
  • Time management and efficiency — Optimizing work speed without compromising quality, production planning, and deadline management
  • Customer communication and requirements gathering — Understanding client specifications, design preferences, fabric choices, and delivery timelines
  • Building a portfolio — Documenting completed work, photographing embroidery pieces, and creating a professional portfolio for clients and employers
  • Career pathways in embroidery — Employment opportunities, self-employment options, entrepreneurship models, and skill advancement to master level
  • Digital tools for embroidery business — Using mobile apps for order management, design inspiration from Pinterest/Instagram, and online marketing basics
  • Indian embroidery market trends — Current demand for wedding wear, ethnic fashion, export opportunities, and sustainable embroidery practices

  • Execute Mirror/Shisha embroidery with proper mirror attachment, decorative stitching, and design placement on ethnic garments
  • Master traditional Chikankari stitches including backstitch, running stitch, eyelet work, and French knots with consistent quality

💼 Career Paths

Embroidery Artisan (Home-Based)
Work from home taking custom orders from wedding wear designers, boutiques, and online clients. Build repeat customer base and scale to hiring assistants.
Embroidery Workshop Supervisor
Manage embroidery production in fashion studios, wedding wear shops, and textile units. Oversee quality, train junior staff, and meet client deadlines.
Wedding Wear Embroidery Specialist
Specialize in bridal lehenga, saree, and suit embroidery. Work with high-end designers or run independent bridal embroidery studio. High earning potential during wedding seasons.
Export-Quality Embroidery Producer
Produce embroidered garments for export to UK, USA, Gulf countries. Meet international quality standards. Supply to fashion export houses and online global platforms.
Ethnic Fashion Brand Embroidery Lead
Work with ethnic wear brands (Biba, Libas, Kalki Fashion) managing embroidery design and production. Oversee seasonal collections and quality assurance.
Online Embroidery Business Owner
Sell embroidered pieces on Etsy, Amazon Handmade, Instagram, and own website. Create niche products (customized dupattas, saree borders, accessories) with high margins.
Embroidery Trainer/Instructor
Teach embroidery in vocational institutes, community centers, or online platforms. Create video tutorials. Conduct workshops for NGOs and skill development programs.
Garment Factory Embroidery Technician
Work in large apparel manufacturing units handling embroidery for bulk orders. Ensure consistency, manage inventory, and maintain production schedules.
